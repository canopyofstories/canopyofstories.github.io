People who commute by bike, experience this perplexing phenomenon every single day. Bike lanes abruptly disappear. Painted lines fade out, only to come back again at traffic stops. Like a tokenistic bone, thrown with a "here". And the moment that light turns green, you're on your own again. The lanes that do exist, exist threateningly close to cars travelling at 80km/hr where one stray tug could send a biker flying. With laws that make it illegal to cycle on sidewalks above a certain age, and because the only infrastructure you're left with is disjointed, or nonexistent, the message is overwhelmingly clear.

CYCLING TO WORK has been proven to increase overall wellbeing by reducing stress levels and disease risks, while providing the sustainability of integrated physical activity into one's daily routine.

It also decreases weekly expenses, while greatly reducing one's carbon footprint. This quickly compounds as a great means of reducing carbon emissions, if more people saw cycling as a viable means of transportation. For others, it's the only viable means of transportation they can afford.

When surveying the barriers to biking, the lack of safe cycling infrastructure is consistently listed as a chief deterrent.

BIKING ROUTES don't need to look like half-hazard paint lines, fighting for road real estate. New biking infrastructure is a wonderful opportunity for collaborative, sustainable, community-oriented design. It can look like lush greenways, woven through parks. It can be bike paths connecting shopping districts and community centres. Bike routes on roads, where the lane is separated from traffic and lined with small bollards, away from the threat of swinging car-parked doors, is the only road bike lane that makes sense.
